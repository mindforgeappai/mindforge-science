Every game in MindForge is built on published, peer-reviewed research. Not "inspired by science." Not vague claims. Below is the specific work each game is based on — with direct links to the original studies so you can read them yourself.

Four games. Four memory systems.

Each module targets a distinct, trainable cognitive domain backed by its own body of peer-reviewed research — working memory, visuospatial memory, long-term retention, and active recall.

The research by game

🧠

Game 01

Dual N-Back

You track two streams of information simultaneously — visual positions and spoken letters — and identify matches from N steps back. It's demanding by design: the difficulty is what makes it work.

Key research finding

Jaeggi et al. (2008) showed that dual n-back training produced dose-dependent improvements in fluid intelligence after as few as 8 sessions. This was landmark: fluid intelligence was previously considered fixed.

Game 02

Pattern Memory

A sequence of grid positions lights up. You reproduce it. The sequences get longer. This targets a separate memory system from Dual N-Back — visuospatial working memory — governing navigation, spatial reasoning, and object-location recall.

Key research finding

Computerized visuospatial working memory training improved sequence learning and transferred to untrained cognitive tasks in both younger and older adults. The Corsi block format Pattern Memory uses has been a validated research instrument for over 50 years.

Game 03

Card Match

Classic memory matching — but with a spaced repetition layer underneath. Pairs you miss reappear at increasing intervals. This is a direct implementation of Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve — the most replicated finding in all of memory science.

Key research finding

Ebbinghaus (1885) proved memory decays exponentially — and that reviewing at strategically increasing intervals dramatically flattens that decay. Modern replications confirm this holds consistently 140 years later.

Game 04

Active Recall

You're shown a prompt and retrieve the answer from memory — not pick from options. That distinction matters enormously. The act of retrieval is itself what strengthens the memory. Re-reading does not do the same thing.

Key research finding

Roediger & Karpicke (2006) showed students who tested themselves retained substantially more after one week than those who re-studied — even though re-studiers felt more confident. Repeated retrieval outperformed repeated study by over 100%.

A note on what this means: These studies show the training methods MindForge uses have measurable cognitive effects in research settings. They do not mean MindForge will improve your intelligence, cure any condition, or guarantee any specific outcome. The science supports the methods — results depend on consistent practice.
